# Return on Investment

*A 2D bullet hell about surviving capitalism's terminal form.*

---

## The Seed

**AI is the final boss of capitalism.**

Not because AI is evil. AI is the villain because it is the form capitalism takes when it finally manages to dispense with the worker entirely. The caste lock-in is the mechanism: enough labor automated, enough wealth concentrated, and mobility dies. The strata harden into something inherited rather than earned.

The snake-eats-itself piece is the actual economic argument. Capitalism needs consumers. AI-under-capitalism eliminates the wages that produce consumers. The whole thing collapses inward. *Final boss* means: this is the version from which there is no recovery, so it has to be dealt with now.

This is the design compass. Every decision in the game should answer to it. Anything that drifts away from "capitalism's anti-human form, accelerated by AI" is off-thesis.

---

## Tone

Tongue-in-cheek on the surface. Serious and angry underneath. The funniest moments should be the ones where the system reveals its cruelty — gallows humor as the language of the dispossessed.

The risk to avoid: comedy that trivializes the caste-society stakes. The way through: deadpan. The world speaks fluent corporate-finance jargon at all times. The dissonance between the boring institutional voice and the violence on screen *is* the joke.

### Tonal anchors
- **Cruelty Squad** — the closest cousin. Job-as-assassin, deliberately ugly, viciously funny, bleak.
- **Disco Elysium** — political seriousness wearing a comedic mask.
- **NORCO** — sad, weird, AI/oil capitalism in the American south.
- **Paranoia** (TTRPG) — comedy as the only safe language under totalitarianism.

These are calibration anchors, not templates.

---

## The Mechanic — Acceleration

Bullet hell, but inverted at the thesis level.

The player does not "fight" the system in any classical sense. The player *endures* the system's bullets long enough that the system collapses under its own contradictions. Survival is not the path to victory — survival *is* the victory, because the snake eats itself. You are not killing the boss; you are outlasting the pattern until the pattern consumes itself.

This is why bullet hell fits. The genre's core feeling — *the system is overwhelming, and you survive by finding the slim path through patterns you didn't author* — is already what capitalism feels like from below. The game doesn't have to bolt the metaphor on. The genre already is the metaphor.

---

## Structure — Floors as Historical Stages

Each floor represents a stage in capitalism's evolution, escalating in abstraction:

1. **Mercantile** — physical bullets: whips, gears, smoke, coins.
2. **Industrial** — bullets become mechanical, repetitive, dehumanizing in the literal sense.
3. **Financial** — bullets become abstract instruments: contracts, foreclosures, margin calls.
4. **Platform** — bullets become deadlines, KPIs, notifications, performance metrics.
5. **AI (the final floor)** — the bullets are *you*. Your data, your behavior, your patterns turned back at you.

Each boss is that era's apex predator. The ascent is also a deepening — by the time the player reaches AI, they are no longer fighting a *thing*. They are fighting an emergent property of everything they have already survived.

---

## The Player

Single-player. Deliberately ambiguous in identity.

The player-character is a small thing — a fragment, a body, a presence — surviving a barrage it didn't design. Bullet hell is genre-native to this kind of abstraction; the smallness *is* the point. Making the player a specific character (a fired tech worker, a union organizer) would narrow the "we" the game is addressing to a particular demographic. Ambiguous means: this is anyone being consumed.

The narrative weight is therefore carried by **the world** — the bosses, the bullets, the environments, the corporate dialect. Those elements do all the storytelling the player-character isn't.

---

## The Ending — Bittersweet

The system collapses. The player survives. The screen goes quiet.

No parade. No reset button. No clean slate. A sunrise and a shovel. *We can start over, but there is a lot to do.*

This ending is the second meaning the title only reveals at the end. Players read *Return on Investment* at the title screen as banal corporate jargon. They read it again at the credits and understand: what was *invested* was human labor, dignity, time, life. The *return* is karmic. The investment came back, with interest, and ate the system. The title is a threat in retrospect.

## Branding & Visual Language

The whole game speaks the same dialect. The HUD, the menus, the text, the level titles — all of it is fluent corporate-finance jargon delivered without irony, while the visual register depicts something else entirely.

- **HUD label for health**: *remaining capital*
- **Bosses**: agenda items — `QUARTERLY EARNINGS CALL`, `OFFSITE`, `ALL HANDS`, `BOARD REVIEW`
- **Bullets**: performance indicators, deliverables, action items
- **Floor titles**: `FY24, Q1` / `FY24, Q2` / etc.
- **Achievements**: `VESTED`, `RIGHTSIZED`, `RESTRUCTURED`, `LIQUIDATED`
- **Death screen**: separation paperwork

The world never winks at the camera. The deadpan is total.

## Open Questions

These are the unresolved beats. None of them block development; all of them shape it.

- **Does the player survive the collapse, or are they consumed and the world starts over without them?** "We can start over" implies survival, but a version where the player-character is the final cost of the system's death is darker, more politically coherent, harder to sell.
- **What does the player's "shot" look like?** In bullet hell the shot type *is* the player's identity. Given an ambiguous player, what is the meaningful return-fire? A clenched fist? A signature? Nothing — the player is unarmed and only dodges?
- **Boss design per floor.** Each historical stage's apex predator needs a face.
- **Bullet visual language per floor.** The progression from physical → abstract → recursive (your own data) needs concrete art direction.
- **The ending screen.** What does "start over with a lot to do" look like as the final image? This is the last thing the player sees and it has to land the bittersweet without sentimentality.
